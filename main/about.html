Imagine measuring someone’s heart rate without even being in the same room as them...




Technology can revolutionize how we extract and analyze biometric information.

The face flushes slightly with each pump of blood—over time to reveal a person's heart rate. Our application applies this theory in order to make a technology that can detect a user's heart beat and requires nothing more than a video. It can handle moving faces and both dark and light skin tones in natural conditions: subjects can move about naturally, and no special lighting is required. The application works in real time (~15 seconds) and is accurate to within 5 beats per minute.

This work has the potential to revolutionize medical care, especially in developing countries where more people have smart phones than access to medical care, and it has many security and defense applications: detecting spoofed faces, finding live soldiers who need to be evacuated from battlefields, and analyzing the emotions of subjects during polygraphs, high-stakes meetings, or media appearances.



This demonstration was developed by Carnegie Mellon University students Katie Williams (Information Systems and Human-Computer Interaction '18), Divya Mohan (Information Systems and Human-Computer Interaction '18), and Asawari Kanitkar (Information Systems '18).
